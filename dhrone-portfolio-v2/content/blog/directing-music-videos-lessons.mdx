## Every shot has a job

On a video set, a shot that doesn't move the story forward gets cut, no matter how good it looks in isolation. The same discipline applies to a landing page. Every section needs a job — building trust, explaining value, or driving an action — and if it isn't doing that job, it's just decoration competing for attention.

## Pacing is a UX skill

A music video lives or dies on pacing: how long you hold a shot, when you cut, when you let a moment breathe. Interfaces have the same rhythm problem. Too many competing animations and the page feels frantic. Too few transitions and it feels lifeless. The goal in both mediums is the same — guide attention without the audience noticing they're being guided.

## Color grading and brand identity

Color grading a video isn't just making it "look nice" — it's reinforcing tone and identity in a way the audience feels before they consciously register it. That's exactly what a considered color palette does for a website. A consistent accent color used deliberately across buttons, highlights, and hover states does the same job a color grade does in a final cut: it makes the whole piece feel intentional rather than assembled.

## Bringing it back to the screen

The biggest thing I carried over from directing into building software is restraint. The best edit usually isn't the one with the most cuts and effects — it's the one where every choice earns its place. The best interface isn't the one with the most features and animations — it's the one where every element is there on purpose. Different tools, same instinct.
